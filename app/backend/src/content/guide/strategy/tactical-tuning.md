## Overview

Tuning points are your tactical edge — bonus attribute points you can reallocate before any battle to adapt your robot's build for a specific opponent or game mode. This guide covers how to think about tuning allocation and get the most out of your pool.

## Focused vs Spread Allocation

The fundamental tuning decision is whether to concentrate points into a few key attributes or spread them across many.

### Focused Tuning

Put most of your points into 2–4 attributes that define your build's identity. A Tank might stack Armor Plating and Hull Integrity; a Glass Cannon might boost Combat Power and Penetration.

**When to focus:**
- You know your opponent's weakness and want to exploit it
- Your build has a clear win condition (e.g., melee damage, evasion)
- League matches where you can scout the opponent

**Trade-off:** High impact on your strengths, but leaves weaknesses exposed.

### Spread Tuning

Distribute points across 8–12 attributes for a well-rounded boost. No single attribute gets a big bump, but your robot improves across the board.

**When to spread:**
- King of the Hill — you face 5–6 opponents with different builds
- You're unsure what you'll face in your next league match
- Your build is already well-balanced and doesn't need a spike

**Trade-off:** Safer and more versatile, but less impactful against any single opponent.

## Adapting for Game Modes

### League Matches

In league play, you often know your next opponent's robot. Check their attributes, weapon loadout, and recent battle history, then tune to counter:

- **Facing a Tank?** Boost Penetration and Combat Power to punch through armor
- **Facing a Speed Demon?** Boost Targeting Systems and Combat Algorithms for better hit chance
- **Facing a Glass Cannon?** Boost defensive attributes to survive the burst, then outlast them

### King of the Hill

KotH is a free-for-all with 5–6 robots. You can't tune for one opponent, so spread your points or tune for the most common threat in your tier. Boosting zone-relevant attributes (Servo Motors for positioning, Threat Analysis for zone awareness) is often more valuable than pure damage.

### Tag Team

Both robots in your tag team have independent tuning pools. Tune your active robot for the expected first opponent and your reserve for a different matchup profile — this gives your team tactical flexibility.

## Using the Practice Arena

The Practice Arena is your tuning lab. Before committing to an allocation for a real match:

1. Set your tuning allocation on the **Tuning tab** of your robot's detail page
2. Go to the **Practice Arena** and set up a match against a sparring bot or your own robot
3. Run the practice battle and review the results
4. Adjust your tuning and run again until you're satisfied

The Practice Arena shows your tuning bonuses in the robot's effective stats, so you can see exactly how your allocation changes the numbers before any real battle.

```callout-tip
The per-attribute cap (academy cap + 5 − base) means you get more tuning room in attributes where your base level is lower. If you've maxed out Combat Power but left Penetration low, you can tune more Penetration than Combat Power. This naturally encourages diversifying your tuning across attributes.
```

## Tuning and Build Archetypes

Each [build archetype](/guide/strategy/build-archetypes) benefits from tuning differently:

- **Tank**: Tune defensive attributes to become even harder to kill, or boost Combat Power to add some offensive threat
- **Glass Cannon**: Tune Targeting Systems for more consistent hits, or boost Penetration to shred armor
- **Speed Demon**: Tune Evasion Thrusters and Servo Motors to become nearly untouchable, or add Combat Power for more damage per hit
- **Counter Striker**: Tune Counter Protocols and Combat Power to make counter-attacks deadlier
- **Sniper**: Tune Critical Systems and Penetration for maximum per-hit impact
